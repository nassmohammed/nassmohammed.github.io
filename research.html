Research Focus

My research lies at the intersection of dark matter physics and galactic evolution. As a PhD student in the David A. Dunlap Department of Astronomy & Astrophysics, I apply a combination of observational data and theoretical models to understand the properties of dark matter through the study of stellar streams and other galactic structures.

  • Spectroscopic analysis of stellar streams using DESI data
  • Constraining dark matter substructure through stream perturbation modeling
  • Developing new statistical methods for stream characterization
  • Polarized radio emission studies with the CHIME instrument

Stellar Streams as Dark Matter Probes

Stellar streams—the tidally disrupted remnants of globular clusters and dwarf galaxies—offer a unique window into the distribution and nature of dark matter in our galaxy. My work focuses on:

  • Analyzing perturbations in stream morphology as signatures of dark matter subhalos
  • Cross-matching stream stars with spectroscopic surveys to obtain detailed kinematics
  • Developing improved statistical frameworks for mass distribution modeling
  • Comparing observations with predictions from different dark matter models

This research provides constraints on the small-scale structure of dark matter, which can distinguish between cold, warm, and self-interacting dark matter models.

The 'Tadpole' Discovery in Radio Astronomy

The 'tadpole' feature as observed in our polarization data

In my work with the Global Magneto-Ionic Medium Survey (GMIMS) and the CHIME instrument, we discovered a unique polarized radio structure dubbed the 'tadpole'. This research:

  • Characterized a previously unidentified polarized radio feature in our galaxy
  • Developed novel data processing techniques for wide-field polarimetry
  • Provided new constraints on cosmic ray propagation in the interstellar medium
  • Demonstrated the power of new-generation radio telescopes for polarization studies

Our findings were published in Mohammed & Ordog et al. 2024, representing an important contribution to our understanding of the polarized radio sky.
